Portfolio Resources

A portfolio helps employers understand how you think, what you created, and how you solved a problem. For many roles in UX, product, research, and data, a portfolio is one of the clearest ways to show your strengths beyond a resume.

This page provides practical guidance for building a clear and professional portfolio that highlights your contributions, shows your process, and helps recruiters quickly understand your work.

Quick Start: What a Strong Portfolio Should Include

  • A short introduction: explain who you are, what roles you are targeting, and what kinds of projects interest you.
  • Two to four strong projects: quality matters more than quantity.
  • Clear navigation: employers should be able to reach your best work in one or two clicks.
  • Your role and contribution: clearly explain what you did, not only what the team did.
  • Contact information: include your email, LinkedIn, and other relevant links.

How to Write a Strong Case Study

Good case studies are not long essays. They are structured explanations that help readers quickly understand the challenge, your process, your decisions, and the outcome.

Recommended Structure

  1. Problem: What challenge were you solving, and who was the user or stakeholder?
  2. Goal: What did success look like?
  3. Process: What methods did you use, such as research, analysis, prototyping, or testing?
  4. Key Decisions: Why did you choose one direction over another?
  5. Outcome: What changed, what did you deliver, and what was the impact?
  6. Reflection: What did you learn, and what would you improve next time?

Show Evidence Clearly

Include visuals and concrete examples when they help explain your work, such as wireframes, diagrams, prototype screenshots, interview themes, dashboards, or testing findings.

You do not need to include everything. Focus on the pieces that best help someone understand your thinking and your contribution.

Portfolio Ideas by Career Path

UX / Product Design

  • User research summaries
  • Journey maps or task flows
  • Wireframes and prototypes
  • Testing results and design iterations

Research

  • Research question and study design
  • Recruitment approach and interview guide
  • Themes and insights
  • Recommendations and limitations

Data / Analytics

  • Problem statement and dataset context
  • Cleaning and analysis approach
  • Visualizations and key insights
  • Deliverables and takeaways

How Recruiters Usually Review a Portfolio

Many recruiters and hiring managers do not read every word on their first visit. They usually scan quickly to understand who you are, what type of work you do, and whether your projects feel relevant to the role.

First Look

They check your homepage, target role, and project titles to understand your focus.

Project Scan

They look for clear summaries, readable visuals, and signs of process and decision-making.

Deeper Review

They spend more time on projects that feel relevant, well-structured, and easy to follow.

Portfolio Checklist Before You Share

  • Your homepage clearly states your target roles or areas of interest.
  • Projects have short summaries so employers can scan quickly.
  • Each case study explains your role, tools, and timeline when relevant.
  • Images are readable and captions help explain what the viewer is seeing.
  • Links work and your contact information is easy to find.
  • Your strongest project appears first or is clearly highlighted.

Quick Actions: What to Improve This Week

  • Write a clearer introduction: explain what roles you want and what kinds of work you do.
  • Revise one project summary: make it easier to understand in two or three sentences.
  • Add captions to visuals: explain what the reader should notice.
  • Check your links: confirm that every project, image, and button works.

Good Goal for This Week

Focus on making one project much stronger instead of trying to update your whole portfolio at once. One clear and well-explained case study is often more useful than several incomplete ones.

Common Portfolio Mistakes to Avoid

  • Too much text: use headings, bullets, and visuals to make your story easier to scan.
  • Unclear personal contribution: clearly describe what you owned and delivered.
  • Only showing final screens: include process, reasoning, and iteration, not only polished results.
  • No outcomes or takeaways: explain what changed, what you learned, or what impact the project had.
  • Broken navigation: test every link and every page before sharing your site.
