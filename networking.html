Networking and Building Connections

Networking is not about asking strangers for a job. It is about building real professional relationships that help you learn, explore possible roles, and understand how people entered the field. Even one short conversation can help you identify useful skills, new communities, and clearer next steps.

This page gives you practical ways to start networking with confidence through short outreach messages, informational interviews, event preparation, and follow-up strategies.

Quick Start: 3 Steps You Can Do This Week

  1. Find 3 people: Search LinkedIn for UMSI alumni or professionals in roles that interest you.
  2. Send 2 messages: Use one of the outreach templates below to request a short conversation.
  3. Do 1 follow-up: Thank the person, write down your takeaways, and decide on one next step.

Start Networking with Confidence

Simple Elevator Pitch Template

A short introduction can make networking easier. Try this structure and adjust it to your own goals.

  • Who you are: “I’m a UMSI student interested in …”
  • What you have done: “I’ve worked on projects in … / I have experience with …”
  • What you want: “I’m exploring roles in … and would love to learn more.”

Example

“Hi, I’m a UMSI MSI student interested in UX and product design. I’ve worked on projects involving user research and prototyping. I’m currently exploring internship opportunities and would love to learn how you entered the field and what skills you think matter most.”

Why Networking Matters

Networking helps you understand what different jobs are really like, learn from professionals with experience, and build confidence before applying for internships or full-time roles. It is one of the most practical ways to turn career exploration into action.

Outreach Message Templates

LinkedIn Connection Request

“Hi [Name], I’m a UMSI student interested in [field]. I noticed your experience in [role or company] and would be glad to connect. Thank you.”

Informational Interview Request

“Hi [Name], I’m a UMSI student exploring [role or industry]. I came across your background in [specific detail] and would love to learn from your experience. Would you be open to a 15–20 minute informational conversation sometime in the next two weeks? I’m happy to work around your schedule. Thank you.”

Follow-Up Thank You Message

“Thank you again for your time today. I really appreciated your advice on [one takeaway]. My next step is [one action]. If it is okay, I would love to stay in touch and share an update in a few weeks.”

Use the TIARA Method for Informational Interviews

The TIARA method gives you a simple way to ask stronger questions during an informational interview. It helps you move beyond small talk and learn what the role, field, and hiring process are really like.

Trends

Ask what changes are happening in the field and what skills are becoming more important.

Insights

Ask what the person has learned from their own path, including what they wish they knew earlier.

Advice

Ask what a student should focus on now to prepare for similar roles or industries.

Resources

Ask about useful communities, events, websites, courses, or people to follow.

Assignments

Ask what kinds of tasks, projects, or experiences helped them most early in their career.

Strong Informational Interview Questions

  • What does a typical week look like in your role?
  • What skills matter most for entry-level candidates in this field?
  • What kinds of projects or experiences helped you the most early on?
  • What should a student focus on if they want to prepare well for this kind of role?
  • Are there any communities, events, or resources you recommend?
  • What do you think students often misunderstand about this career path?

Networking Events: Before, During, and After

Before the Event

  • Set 1 or 2 goals, such as meeting two people or learning about one role.
  • Prepare a short self-introduction and two questions.
  • Review the speaker list or event details if they are available.

During the Event

  • Ask open questions such as “What brings you here?” or “What do you work on?”
  • Listen for keywords about tools, teams, and skills, then ask thoughtful follow-up questions.
  • Write down names and one memorable detail after each conversation.

After the Event

  • Send a short follow-up message within 24 to 48 hours.
  • Connect on LinkedIn with a short personal note.
  • Track your contacts in a document or spreadsheet so you can follow up later.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Asking for a referral in your first message.
  • Sending a long message without a clear purpose.
  • Forgetting to follow up after someone helps you.
  • Treating networking as a one-time task instead of an ongoing habit.
